**Q: Do I need a buyer's agent?**
A: In Canada, buyer representation is typically free to you — the seller pays the commission. A buyer's agent negotiates on your behalf, gives you access to MLS listings, and guides you through offers and closing. It's strongly recommended, especially in competitive markets.

## Fees & Costs

**Q: What are your fees?**
A: In Canada, real estate commission is typically 3–5% of the sale price, paid by the seller and split between the listing and buyer's brokerage. As a buyer, you generally pay nothing out of pocket for agent services. Want me to connect you with an agent who can explain the specifics for your situation?

**Q: Are there any hidden fees when buying?**
A: Good to plan for! Beyond the purchase price, budget for closing costs of about 1.5–4% of the price. This typically includes land transfer tax (in most provinces), legal fees, title insurance, and any adjustments. Your agent and lawyer will give you an accurate estimate before closing.

## Affordability & Financing

**Q: Can I afford to buy?**
A: It depends on your income, down payment, and current rates — but a mortgage pre-approval is the fastest way to get a clear picture. Most lenders look for a total debt service ratio below 44%. Would you like some guidance on where to start with pre-approval?

**Q: What's a pre-approval?**
A: A pre-approval is a lender's conditional commitment to loan you up to a certain amount. It locks in a rate for 90–120 days and shows sellers you're a serious buyer. It's free and doesn't commit you to anything. We can refer you to a mortgage broker if you'd like.

**Q: What is the FHSA?**
A: The First Home Savings Account (FHSA) lets first-time buyers save up to $40,000 tax-free toward a home purchase. Contributions are tax-deductible and withdrawals for a qualifying home are tax-free. It's one of the best tools available to first-time buyers right now.

**Q: Do I need mortgage insurance?**
A: If your down payment is less than 20% of the purchase price, yes — CMHC mortgage insurance is required by law in Canada. The premium is added to your mortgage (2.8–4% of the loan) and protects the lender, not you. On the upside, it lets you enter the market sooner with as little as 5% down.

## Process & Timeline

**Q: How long does it take to buy a house?**
A: Once your offer is accepted, closing typically takes 30–90 days depending on what you negotiate. Finding the right property usually takes longer — anywhere from a few weeks to several months, depending on the market and your criteria. Starting with a pre-approval and a clear wish list speeds things up significantly.

**Q: What happens after I make an offer?**
A: After an offer is submitted, the seller can accept, reject, or counter. If accepted, you enter the condition period (typically 5–10 business days) where you finalize financing and do your home inspection. Once conditions are waived, it's firm — then you work toward your closing date.

**Q: What is a CMA?**
A: A Comparative Market Analysis (CMA) is a report prepared by an agent that estimates a property's value based on recent comparable sales in the area. It's the standard tool used to price listings and evaluate offers. Royal LePage agents provide them free of charge.
